On 26 June 2024, the World Bank Group announced the 30-month debarment with conditional release of Kenya-based Ernst & Young LLP (EY Kenya). The debarment arose from EY Kenya's involvement in two World Bank-financed programmes in Somalia — the Somali Core Economic Institutions and Opportunities Programme (SCORE) and the Second Public Financial Management Capacity Strengthening Project (PFM II).

EY Kenya was found to have: (1) failed to disclose conflicts of interest during the selection and implementation of four contracts; (2) engaged an undisclosed agent in connection with those contracts; and (3) made provision for irregular allowances to project officials — conduct the World Bank characterised as fraudulent and corrupt practices under its Consultant Guidelines.

The debarment was part of a negotiated settlement under which EY Kenya admitted culpability. Mitigating factors — voluntary cooperation, disciplinary action against responsible staff, and a voluntary bidding moratorium during negotiations — reduced the sanction from a maximum potential period to 30 months.

EY Kenya may be reinstated to WBG project eligibility upon demonstrating full implementation of the integrity compliance programme and continued cooperation with WBG INT.
03 — Root Cause Analysis

Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram — What Went Wrong?

A root cause analysis structures the systemic and proximate failures that enabled the misconduct. The fishbone below maps six causal categories into the central problem: fraudulent practices under World Bank-financed contracts.

Proximate Cause

Failure to disclose conflicts of interest across four contracts, use of an undisclosed agent, and payment of irregular allowances to project officials — all constituting fraudulent and corrupt practices under WBG Consultant Guidelines.

Systemic Cause

Institutional pressure to retain high-value public sector mandates, absence of mandatory COI disclosure controls, and a quality review process that failed to flag red flags — particularly in a fragile-state context with elevated corruption risk.

Contextual Cause

Somalia's governance environment created conditions where insider relationships with officials were exploitable. The multi-stakeholder reporting chain in development finance created opportunities for misconduct to go undetected for years.

Financial Impact

Loss of all World Bank-financed project audit and consulting mandates across the region for 30 months. East Africa is a high-growth market for the Big 4 — development finance mandates (multilateral, bilateral, NGO) represent a significant share of public-sector revenue. Cross-debarment extends the commercial exclusion to AfDB, ADB, EBRD, and IDB-financed projects globally. Legal costs from the WBG proceedings and the subsequent Gathungu litigation (Ksh 43M court award) add direct financial liability.

Reputational Impact

The public nature of a World Bank debarment is by design: maximum deterrence through transparency. For EY Kenya — a firm whose market proposition rests on trust and professional integrity — the reputational damage in both government and multilateral circles is severe. Government procurement frameworks in Kenya and across East Africa routinely check WBG debarment lists. Civil society, donor community, and media scrutiny intensifies. The Gathungu litigation added a secondary public narrative of internal dysfunction.

Impact on Development Projects

The SCORE and PFM II projects were designed to strengthen Somalia's economic governance and revenue systems — essential state-building objectives in a fragile post-conflict environment. Compromised assurance on financial statements creates uncertainty around fund utilisation. Implementing agencies must appoint replacement auditors mid-project at additional cost and with potential delays.

Systemic Sector Signal

EY Kenya's debarment was followed less than two years later by PwC Kenya's debarment in March 2026 — over collusive practices in the Eastern Electricity Highway Project. Two of the Big 4 in East Africa debarred within two years signals a systemic audit quality and ethics crisis, and raises questions about whether the professional services model for development finance requires structural reform.

08 — Strategic Recommendations

Prevention, Recovery & Long-Term Resilience

A. For EY Kenya — Recovery Pathway

01
Execute the WBG Integrity Compliance Programme
Deliver the compliance programme on schedule as a non-negotiable condition of reinstatement. Treat it as an institutional rebuild, not a box-ticking exercise — the quality of implementation will be evaluated by WBG INT.
Immediate
02
Appoint a Chief Compliance Officer for East Africa
Create a dedicated CCO role with direct reporting to EY Africa leadership, empowered to halt bid submissions and flag conflicts without partner interference — removing the structural vulnerability of revenue-focused partners self-policing.
Immediate
03
Rebuild public sector client trust proactively
Engage ICPAK, the Kenyan government, and East African development partners in transparent dialogue about the reforms undertaken. Public accountability — not silence — is the faster path to reputational recovery.
Medium-term
04
Diversify revenue away from MDB-financed mandates
Accelerate growth in private equity due diligence, ESG assurance, climate finance advisory, and fintech audit — segments where reputation is not yet impacted and where East Africa's growth trajectory creates genuine demand.
Medium-term
05
Implement mandatory COI disclosure technology
Deploy a digital conflict-of-interest registry requiring partner attestation before any public or multilateral bid is submitted. System-enforced disclosure eliminates reliance on individual integrity as the sole control.
Medium-term
06
Position post-reinstatement as a compliance differentiator
Upon reinstatement, market the integrity programme not as a remedial requirement but as a genuine competitive advantage — the only Big 4 firm in East Africa to have built and independently verified an MDB-grade compliance framework.
Long-term

B. For the World Bank & Multilateral Donors

07
Reform the auditor appointment model
Currently, audit firms are hired and paid by implementing agencies — creating an inherent conflict. Donors having greater input into auditor selection for high-risk and fragile-state contexts would structurally reduce capture risk.
Long-term
08
Mandate audit firm rotation on long-running projects
Long-term relationships between audit firms and project officials create the conditions for insider capture. Mandatory rotation — aligned with procurement cycles — disrupts the trust networks that enable misconduct.
Medium-term
09
Real-time financial monitoring as a complement to audit
Supplement annual audit assurance with continuous digital financial monitoring tools — particularly in fragile-state contexts where the audit cycle is too slow to detect and deter misconduct in near-real-time.
Long-term

C. For ICPAK & the Audit Profession

10
Establish a public register of MDB-related sanctions
ICPAK should create and maintain a public register of any World Bank or MDB sanctions involving Kenyan-registered firms or certified professionals — independent of whether the firm self-reports.
Medium-term
11
Mandatory MDB ethics training for development finance practitioners
CPD requirements should include jurisdiction-specific training on MDB procurement rules, donor reporting standards, and conflict-of-interest obligations for any CPA working on donor-financed engagements.
Immediate
12
Formalise information sharing with WBG INT
A mutual recognition or information-sharing agreement between ICPAK and the World Bank Integrity Vice Presidency would allow investigation findings to inform domestic professional disciplinary proceedings without duplication of effort.
Long-term

10 — Systemic Observation

The Structural Governance Gap in Development Finance Audit

Core finding: The EY Kenya and PwC Kenya debarments are not isolated ethical failures — they are symptoms of a structural flaw in how development finance audit engagements are designed. Audit firms are hired and paid by implementing agencies (which have an interest in clean audit opinions), while the World Bank and other MDBs rely on those same firms' independence. This creates an inherent conflict that individual ethics controls alone cannot reliably overcome.

The World Bank's response — negotiated settlements, reduced debarments in exchange for cooperation and compliance programmes — represents a pragmatic and evidence-based regulatory approach. The conditional release model incentivises firms to cooperate and self-reform rather than contest findings, reducing the burden on the Sanctions Board and accelerating the rehabilitation timeline.

However, the frequency of Big 4 debarments in East Africa (two within two years) suggests that the deterrence effect of individual sanctions is insufficient without complementary structural reform. The fundamental principal-agent problem — where the entity whose work is being assured controls the selection and payment of the assurer — requires structural, not merely behavioural, solutions.

This case study demonstrates that regulatory risk in professional services is not simply a function of individual misconduct: it is a product of institutional design, incentive structures, and governance architecture. Firms operating in high-risk, high-complexity, multi-stakeholder environments — particularly in development finance — must manage regulatory risk as a strategic priority, not a compliance afterthought.
